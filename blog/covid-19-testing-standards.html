covid 19 testing standards

06 May 2023

By examining samples from your mouth or nose, viral tests seek for a current infection with SARS-CoV-2, the virus that causes COVID-19. Nucleic acid amplification tests (NAATs) and antigen testing are the two primary categories of viral tests. One test type may be advised over another depending on the situation. The FDA's guidelines should be followed for all tests.


The majority of NAATs, including PCR-based tests, are carried out in a laboratory. Whether a person has symptoms or not, they are often the most trustworthy tests. The genetic material from viruses is detected by these tests, and it may remain in your body for up to 90 days following a positive test result. As a result, if you tested positive during the previous 90 days, you should not utilize an NAAT if you have tested positive in the last 90 days. 


Self-tests, often known as at-home tests, are typically antigen tests that may be performed anywhere without a designated testing location. Observe the manufacturer's and the FDA's instructions, including any guidance on the potential frequency of testing. The likelihood that you are free of the virus that causes COVID-19 increases after several negative test results. COVIDtests.gov offers free self-testing kits, as well as the option to buy tests online, at pharmacies, and at retail establishments.

• To view a list of approved tests, you can also go to the FDA's website.
• The labeling for permitted over-the-counter antigen tests states the following: Negative findings should be regarded as presumptive (i.e., initial findings). Negative results should not be considered as the only indicator of SARS-CoV-2 infection.
